3/15/2024

I had a fantastic time volunteering at the Field Museum today, because the guest scientist was an astrochemist/geologist specializing in meteorites, and brought a few specimens out to show to visitors.

With that, I will give a very non-in-depth overview of how to identify a meteorite, in case you ever find one of your own!*

*Check your local laws on meteorites. Some governments label them as property of the state.

Rather than chunks of concrete, these are young meteorites. Note the perfectly circular inclusions.

Not even volcanic rocks from Earth would have this scorched crust.

The outer layers of these older meteorites have been completely burned away, leaving only the metal core. In fact, planets are just very large meteorites. The pattern on the left slice is the result of treatment. If you were an ancient blacksmith, you would be quite happy to be given something like the right to work with.

The pallsite on the left has been polished to a mirror sheen. The crystals are iron-based, hence their rust. The right is an extremely interesting specimen of a fossilized meteorite approximately 4.6 billion years old given the prehistoric squid fossil next to it.

2/16/2024

Book review: Princess Floralinda and the Forty-Flight Tower by Tamsyn Muir

A short book that plays with the archtypical damsel-in-distress from fairytales. Like many of the fairytale subversions I enjoy, it does a great job of playfully poking fun at the usual triteness of the genre while acknowledging and riffing on some of its unexpected truths. Especially interesting in the context of the course of fairy tales I am currently taking. The story itself tends to touch on serious themes and ideas and only dives into one major one that interlinks with those mentioned, but it does it well especially given its length. A great quick read, I would recommend it for maybe a teenager willing to entertain the premise or of course a more playful adult with an interest.

10/13/2023

Book review: The Confidence Game by Maria Konnikova

I enjoyed this a lot! It was very interesting to learn about some of the common logical fallacies people fall into in daily life, and how they can be exploited. I do feel like the delineation between steps of the con got muddled sometimes, and that the examples chosen weren't exact one-to-one demonstrations of those steps. However, it was especially enlightening reading it while playing as and with changelings in a D&D campaign. Maybe that's not the intended use of this book. Anyways, I recommend it for anyone interested in the topic, people who have fallen victim to scams and cons (the book emphasizes frequently that it's rarely your fault), and people who have never or think they'd never get caught up in something like that. I already knew the disclaimer that even smart people can be suckered, but never before have I noticed the flaws in my own mentality so keenly. I'm not sure if I'll be able to consciously correct them, but it was a great read!

Book review: Tao Te Ching by Lao Tzu, translated by Ursula K. Le Guin

While I'm disappointed that I wasn't able to find a copy of the translation I was looking for, I enjoyed Le Guin's commentary! However, I tried to not read too much into it and come up with my own interpretations of the text. I jumped around in the text based on what themes I thought were relevant for the day. It's a lot about going with the flow and accepting things as they are, which is good to think about during stressful times in life. While not applicable to everything, I do think my state of mind has improved having read it. As for which translation is best, I'd have to read more translations!

07/21/2023

Book review: Gory Details by Erika Engelhaupt

I enjoyed this book a lot! As I've gotten older and more busy I appreciate books with more episodic concepts that are easy to put down and pick back up, and the focus on a new story each time was engaging. I enjoyed the tieback of every story at the epilogue where the purpose of studying and cataloguing these discoveries was acknowledged as necessary for people to learn and have a healthier attitude towards the world around us and ourselves. Overall, highly recommend if you aren't squeamish and are interested in more bizarre scientific discoveries being made, or if you are squeamish and want to build your tolerance. There are no images and descriptions are kept fairly brief, but there are more detailed drawings to section off the book.
